1. The Favorite Look

Classic Chrome (14 recipes) and Classic Negative (13 recipes) are the undisputed kings.

Why these two?

  • Classic Chrome (The Journalist): It mimics Kodachrome. By separating color intensity from luminance, it creates a mature, "National Geographic" documentary look.
  • Classic Negative (The Artist): It mimics Superia 200. It introduces hard contrast and cyan shifts in shadows, perfect for usage in gritty street photography.

2. The Color of Memory (White Balance)

The data shows a massive preference for Warm/Vintage tones over accurate ones.

Collective Wisdom: Why Warm?

  • ‚óè The "Golden Hour" Bias (Top-Left): Most recipes shift Red +2 and Blue -4. This mimics the warm glow of late afternoon sun.
  • Psychology: Experiments show humans associate warm colors with "safe/happy" memories (nostalgia), while cool colors feel "clinical/modern". Fuji users aren't documenting reality; they are documenting memories.

3. Protecting the Sky (Dynamic Range)

DR400 is the overwhelmingly dominant setting.

Why DR400?

Digital sensors clip highlights instantly (pure white blob). Film rolls off gently. DR400 forces the camera to underexpose and boost shadows, simulating that gentle film roll-off. It's the "Anti-Digital-Clip" button.

4. Texture is King (Grain Effect)

Strong grain is the favorite. Why add noise on purpose?

Collective Wisdom: The "Vinyl" of Photography

  • Digital = Perfect = Boring: 40MP sensors are too clean. They look sterile.
  • Grain = Texture: Adding "Strong" grain breaks up the perfect digital gradients. It gives the eye something to "chew on". It mimics the physical chemical structure of film.

5. The Dream vs. The Delay (Clarity)

Users clearly favor Negative Clarity (-2) despite the processing cost.

Collective Wisdom: The "Mist" Look

  • Bloom Effect: Negative clarity bleeds highlight brightness into shadows. It looks like a diffuse mist filter.
  • The "Video" Look: Modern cinema lenses are often slightly soft. Digital cameras are clinically sharp. Clarity -2 bridges that gap.

6. Color Chrome vs. Saturation

Users boost saturation (+2) to compensate for Color Chrome "Strong".

Collective Wisdom: Richness > Neon

If you just boost color, it looks neon and cheap. If you use Color Chrome Effect, it darkens the colors as they get saturated. The result involves Rich, Deep Reds rather than "Fire Engine" reds.

7. The Hardware (X-Trans IV vs V)

The X100V (IV) remains the dominant creative tool, but the VI is rising.

Collective Wisdom: 26MP vs 40MP

  • X-Trans IV (26MP): Often cited as having "cleaner" high-ISO noise.

8. The "Noise Floor" (ISO)

Why do so many recipes cap Auto-ISO at 6400?

  • ISO 6400: On older sensors, this was unusable. On the X100V/VI's BSI sensor, ISO 6400 noise looks remarkably monochromatic (like film grain) rather than colorful (like digital noise).
  • Freedom: Setting a high limit allows you to shoot at f/8 (for sharp street shots) even at night.
