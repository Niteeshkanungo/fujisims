1. The Favorite Look

Classic Chrome (14 recipes) and Classic Negative (13 recipes) are the undisputed kings.

Technical Analysis

  • Classic Chrome: Mimics Kodachrome. Separation of color intensity from luminance gives a mature, understated look.
  • Classic Negative: Mimics Superia 200/400. Adds cyan shifts in shadows for "street photography" grit.

2. The Psychology of Warmth

The data reveals a massive preference for Warm/Vintage tones (Red+, Blue-).

Technical Analysis

  • The Nostalgia Curve: Psychologically, warm tones equate to memory and comfort. Cool tones feel clinical.
  • Color Grading: The strong push towards +Red neutralizes Fuji's magenta shift, while -Blue adds cinematic yellow grading.

3. Protecting Highlights

DR400 is dominant because it fundamentally changes how the sensor operates.

Technical Analysis

  • Highlight Roll-off: DR400 forces a 2-stop underexposure of the RAW file.
  • Film Emulation: Film handles overexposure gracefully; digital clips instantly. DR400 is the only way to mimic that behavior.

4. Texture vs. Noise

Strong Grain is the overwhelming favorite.

Technical Analysis

  • Texture: Grain masks the clinical perfection of modern 26MP/40MP sensors.
  • Perceived Sharpness: Paradoxically, random noise patterns add high-frequency texture that brains interpret as detail.

5. The "Mist Filter" Effect

Users accept the slowdown of Clarity to achieve a specific look.

Technical Analysis

  • Negative Clarity: Bleeds highlights into shadows, creating a "bloom" effect similar to a ProMist diffusion filter.
  • The Trade-off: Users prefer the "Cinematic" soft look (negative clarity) over the "HDR" gritty look (positive clarity).
